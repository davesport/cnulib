Databases, explained!

Databases are like grocery stores. If you feel comfortable using one, you really can use them all. There are lots of different grocery stores, but overall they're mostly the same. But then why is it still weird walking into a new grocery store for the first time? Everything is slightly out of order. But with a little extra attention you still can find your way around.

See it in action: Example database search

Watch this video to see how a simple database search works. This can help you get started, especially if you aren't familiar with the CNU databases. Keep in mind that this isn't the only way to search, there are lots of ways to find materials in the databases. And let us know if you would like help! We have research appointments, workshops & desk hours for students, specifically to help with research.

Here are the steps taken in this video:

  • Choose a database.
  • Use one or two keywords from your general topic for your first search.
  • Use your results list to find ideas for keywords, potential subtopics, and directions for your research.
  • Assess the quality of your chosen article.

Frequently Asked Questions

These are the questions we get the most, it should help to know you're not alone!

  • What do I do if the article I want isn't available?

    Good news, you have lots of options!

    • Search in Ovid. It has a full-text feature that finds searches for an article in all 15 databases.
    • Google Scholar. Some articles are available in open-access journals on the web. This is good for instant results.
    • Interlibrary loan. Send us the basic info for your article and we will find it! It can take up to 3 days to get your article, often faster.

  • How do I find relevant articles?

    There are some easy ways to find articles that are relevant to your research topic. Here are some:

    • Try multiple keywords, and synonyms to those keywords. The specific term you use matters, and can "unlock" relevant articles.
    • Find important terms used to describe your ideal topic by reading in an encyclopedia, or by reading the titles of articles in your database results page.
    • If you find one relevant article, look through its citation list. Many of the articles it references will also be relevant to your research topic.

  • What database should I use?

    It depends on what you need. If you need scholarly articles, or ebooks, or multimedia & field resources, use the filter above to find the right databases. Remember, it's a good idea to try a couple of databases and see what different results they bring.

  • My project is due tomorrow and I am behind. What should I do?

    We don't recommend working last-minute, but sometimes a project or assignment can sneak up on you. Our advice: go through the same steps as you would normally, and work efficiently. Choosing a topic, finding sources, and writing can be done quickly if you stay focused on the outcome of your research. What should your finished model look like?Keep a clear mind, and you can get this done!

  • How do I learn more about my topic?

    Try an encyclopedia, like Wikipedia or scholarpedia. Alternatively, you can try a simple Google search of your topic, and cross-reference the facts you read. You'll want to check multiple sites to verify the information you find so that you can be confident you're getting the right content.

  • How do I edit my writing?

    This is all about reflection. If you don't know where to start, try to explain your writing and your ideas to a friend or someone who wants to listen. Do you find yourself getting stuck in your explanation? (You can also explain your writing in your head to someone you admire.)
    This is also a great opportunity to make a research appointment in the library! We can look at your work and give you pointers. We don't do the work for you, but we'll help guide you where you want to go.

  • I need to cut down my word count. How do I do this?

    Look over your work and see what is relevant to your thesis. Is there something you've included that doesn't quite fit (even if it's really interesting)?
    Another common writing pattern to look for is overexplanation. Do you spend many sentences building up to the final thought, and is this necessary? Is there an explanation that can be trimmed down and simplified?

  • I need to make my paper longer. How do I do that?

    Revisit your instructions and your thesis. Is there an aspect of your topic that you haven't included yet? Adding relevant material will make your paper both longer and stronger.

  • I have a strange source, how do I cite it?

    We recommend visiting online citation guides for strange sources. We have links to several different options in our formatting section. There are lots of different citation guides that clearly list strange, rare source types and explain how they are cited. Rather than feel pressure to memorize how different sources are cited, revisit your favorite citation guide to ensure you are accurate in making your citations. Visit our formatting section for more options.

  • I don't like formatting and I really struggle with it, how can I make it easier?

    It's okay to not enjoy formatting your paper. There are many ways to improve at it and become more comfortable with it: online citation guides, sample papers, and citation apps. Zotero and Mendeley are apps for your computer that store article PDFs and generate citations in different styles for you. Regardless of which app you choose, make sure its citations are correct. Visit our formatting section for more options.
